\textbf{Introduction} This paper proposes a formal proposal to 
model the tonal processes resulting from vowel deletion. 
This paper proposes that tonal effects triggered by vowel 
deletion can be reduced to two operations over association 
relations: \textit{deassociation} and \textit{reassociation},
which is consistent with the core idea of autosegmental phonology
\citep{goldsmith1976} that prioeritize association lines modifcation
rtanther rhan segmentalfeatural change. We show that these two 
operations derive the major typological outcomes of vowel deletion, 
and extend to less common cases such as vowel deletion blocking. 
This provides a unified and computationally explicit account of 
tonal processes that preserves structural representations while 
modifying only association relations.

\textbf{Typology} \citet{kao2017phonological} conducted a 
typological survey of 30 languages that exhibit vowel deletion 
as a strategy for hiatus resolution, along with the resulting 
tonal processes. When the vowel is deleted, the original tone
that the deleted vowel is associated will undergo some re-arrangement
due to the unassociated relation. The common cases are: 1) get deleted;
2) get retented - either forma a contour with the adjacent tone or 
replace the neightbor tone. See Table 1.

\textbf{Logical Transduction of tonal process triggered by 
vowel-deletion} 



\citet{yolyan2025logical} formalises the 
phonological mappings as logical transductions over 
relational structures. ARs are encoded using a signature 
consisting of unary and binary predicates $M:=\{D|H,L,V,C, 
\lhd_s, \lhd_T, \alpha\}$ As shown in Fig, these predicates 
define the internal structure of ARs. Individual structures 
can be viewed as models over this signature, where elements 
are indexed and interpreted by the predicates. 
For instance, a sequence such as H.L can be represented as a structure 
with two tone elements ordered by $\lhd_T$, each associated via $\alpha$ 
to corresponding vowels.

Phonological mappings are then modeled as \textbf{transformations} 
between an input structure to an output structure. The output 
model is defined by a new interpretation of the predicates. C
rucially, most structural relations 
remain unchanged; the primary modification concerns the association 
relation $\alpha$.

Formally, a logical transduction defines output predicates in terms of 
input predicates:

\begin{align*}
H'(x) &:= H(x) \\
L'(x) &:= L(x) \\
V'(x) &:= V(x) \\
\lhd_T'(x,y) &:= \lhd_T(x,y) \\
\lhd_S'(x,y) &:= \lhd_S(x,y) \\
\alpha'(x,y) &:= \bigl(\alpha(x,y) \land \neg \textsf{deassoc}(x,y)\bigr)
                \lor \textsf{reassoc}(x,y)
\end{align*}

A core insight of autosegmental phonology \citep{goldsmith1976} is that 
many tonal processes are more naturally modeled as modifications to 
association relations rather than changes to segmental or featural 
content. Building on this view, we treat association relations as being 
governed by two operations: \textit{deassociation} and \textit{reassociation}, 
defined below. In addition, the choice of which vowel is deleted or 
survives is language-specific. Table~1 lists three common typological 
patterns together with their logical formalizations. In what follows, we 
refer to these conditions simply as \textsf{delete\_V} and 
\textsf{survive\_V}.

\begin{align*}
\textsf{survive\_V}(x) &:= \exists x' \bigl(\textsf{delete\_V}(x') \land (\lhd(x,x') \lor \lhd(x',x))\bigr) \\
\textsf{deassoc}(x,y) &:= \alpha(x,y) \land \textsf{delete\_V}(y) \\
\textsf{reassoc}(x,y) &:= \neg \alpha(x,y) \land \textsf{survive\_V}(y) \land
\exists y' \bigl(\textsf{delete\_V}(y') \land \alpha(x,y')\bigr)
\end{align*}

Thus, the transduction preserves all structural relations except for 
$\alpha$, which is updated through deassociation and reassociation. 
This captures the intuition that tonal processes triggered by vowel 
deletion operate by modifying association relations rather than altering 
segmental or tonal features.

\section{Introduction}

Vowel hiatus is commonly resolved through processes such as vowel deletion or modification. In tonal languages, where vowels serve as tone-bearing units (TBUs), hiatus resolution often triggers changes in tone–vowel association. These include tone deletion, contour formation, and tone coalescence. While such patterns are difficult to capture in linear phonology, they are naturally represented in autosegmental phonology, where tones and TBUs are represented on separate tiers connected by association relations.

This paper proposes that tonal effects triggered by vowel deletion can be reduced to two operations over association relations: \textit{deassociation} and \textit{reassociation}. We formalize these operations as logical transductions over autosegmental representations. We show that these two operations derive the major typological outcomes of vowel deletion, and extend to less common cases such as vowel deletion blocking. This provides a unified and computationally explicit account of tonal processes that preserves structural representations while modifying only association relations.

\section{Typology}

\citet{kao2017phonological} conducted a typological survey of 30 
languages that exhibit vowel deletion as a strategy for hiatus 
resolution, along with the resulting tonal processes. Of these, 
28 are Niger-Congo languages and 2 are Nilo-Saharan. The survey
finds that when vowel deletion leads to tone loss, 
languages tend to preserve H tone or maintain faithfulness to 
underlying associations. In cases of tone retention, contour 
formation is the dominant outcome, although some languages 
exhibit tonal coalescence as an alternative pattern.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Tone deletion}: arises from deassociation without reassociation.
\item \textbf{Contour formation}: arises from reassociation of a tone to an already-associated TBU.
\item \textbf{Tone coalescence}: arises from reassociation combined with constraints on tonal sequences.
\end{enumerate}

These patterns follow from how association relations are updated after vowel deletion. Importantly, no changes to tonal or segmental features are required.

\section{Logical Transduction Framework}

Following \citet{yolyan2025logical}, we model phonological mappings as 
logical transductions over relational structures. Autosegmental 
representations are encoded using a signature consisting of unary and 
binary predicates:

\begin{align*}
H(x), L(x) &\quad \text{tone predicates} \\
V(x) &\quad \text{vowel predicate} \\
C(x) &\quad \text{consonant predicate} \\
\lhd_T(x,y) &\quad \text{linear ordering on the tone tier} \\
\lhd_S(x,y) &\quad \text{linear ordering on the segment tier} \\
\alpha(x,y) &\quad \text{association relation}
\end{align*}

These predicates define the internal structure of autosegmental 
representations. Individual structures can be viewed as models over this 
signature, where elements are indexed and interpreted by the predicates. 
For instance, a sequence such as H.L can be represented as a structure 
with two tone elements ordered by $\lhd_T$, each associated via $\alpha$ 
to corresponding vowels.

\input{ARmodels.tex}

Phonological mappings are then modeled as transformations between such 
structures. Given an input model, the output model is defined by a new 
interpretation of the predicates. Crucially, most structural relations 
remain unchanged; the primary modification concerns the association 
relation $\alpha$.

Formally, a logical transduction defines output predicates in terms of 
input predicates:

\begin{align*}
H'(x) &:= H(x) \\
L'(x) &:= L(x) \\
V'(x) &:= V(x) \\
\lhd_T'(x,y) &:= \lhd_T(x,y) \\
\lhd_S'(x,y) &:= \lhd_S(x,y) \\
\alpha'(x,y) &:= \bigl(\alpha(x,y) \land \neg \textsf{deassoc}(x,y)\bigr)
                \lor \textsf{reassoc}(x,y)
\end{align*}

A core insight of autosegmental phonology \citep{goldsmith1976} is that 
many tonal processes are more naturally modeled as modifications to 
association relations rather than changes to segmental or featural 
content. Building on this view, we treat association relations as being 
governed by two operations: \textit{deassociation} and \textit{reassociation}, 
defined below. In addition, the choice of which vowel is deleted or 
survives is language-specific. Table~1 lists three common typological 
patterns together with their logical formalizations. In what follows, we 
refer to these conditions simply as \textsf{delete\_V} and 
\textsf{survive\_V}.

\begin{align*}
\textsf{survive\_V}(x) &:= \exists x' \bigl(\textsf{delete\_V}(x') \land (\lhd(x,x') \lor \lhd(x',x))\bigr) \\
\textsf{deassoc}(x,y) &:= \alpha(x,y) \land \textsf{delete\_V}(y) \\
\textsf{reassoc}(x,y) &:= \neg \alpha(x,y) \land \textsf{survive\_V}(y) \land
\exists y' \bigl(\textsf{delete\_V}(y') \land \alpha(x,y')\bigr)
\end{align*}

Thus, the transduction preserves all structural relations except for 
$\alpha$, which is updated through deassociation and reassociation. 
This captures the intuition that tonal processes triggered by vowel 
deletion operate by modifying association relations rather than altering 
segmental or tonal features.

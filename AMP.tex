\textbf{Introduction} This paper proposes a formal proposal to 
model the tonal processes resulting from vowel deletion. We argue
that such processes can be reduced to two operations over 
association relations: \textit{deassociation} and 
\textit{reassociation}, which is consistent with the core idea 
of autosegmental phonology \citep{goldsmith1976}. We show that 
these two operations derive the major typological outcomes of 
vowel deletion, and can possibly extend to less common cases. 
This provides a unified and computationally explicit account 
of tonal processes that preserves structural representations 
while modifying only association relations rather than changing
the segments or features. This also paves the path for learning
more complex tonal processes over multitiered structures.
We focus on three languages that exemplify the major tonal 
patterns triggered by vowel deletion in hiatus: Engenni, 
Estako, and Ogoja Yala \citep{kao2017phonological}. In all 
three languages, the first vowel in the hiatus is deleted, 
but the associated tonal outcomes differ.

\ea 
\textbf{Engenni (tone deletion):} 
Deleting the vowel also removes its associated tone.
\vspace{-1em}
\begin{multicols}{2}
 /`ɔkò \# édèì/ $\rightarrow$ /`ɔkédèì/ \\
 \textit{man canoe} $\rightarrow$ \textit{a man’s canoe} 
 
 /fùnú \# èdhí/ $\rightarrow$ /fùnèdhí/ \\
 \textit{climb palm tree} $\rightarrow$ \textit{to climb a palm tree}
%\end{tabular}
\end{multicols}
\z


\ea \textbf{Estako (contour formation):} 
The tone of the deleted vowel is preserved and reassociates 
to the following vowel, forming a contour with the tone 
originally linked to that vowel.
\vspace{-1em}
\begin{multicols}{2}
/ówà \# ówà/ $\rightarrow$ [ówǒwà] \\
\textit{house house} $\rightarrow$ \textit{every house}

/\`ɔ \# dé \# àkpà/ $\rightarrow$ [\`ɔdâkpà] \\
\textit{he buy cup} $\rightarrow$ \textit{he buys a cup}
\end{multicols}
\z


\ea \textbf{Ogoja Yala (H-tone preservation):} 
Only H tones are preserved. The retained H either replaces 
the tone of the surviving vowel or remains unchanged.
\vspace{-1em}\begin{multicols}{2}
/dè \# áchí/ $\rightarrow$ [dáchí]\\
\textit{give grass} $\rightarrow$ \textit{give grass}

/má \# \`ɔ hí/ $\rightarrow$ [m\` ɔchí]\\
\textit{see tree} $\rightarrow$ \textit{see tree}
\end{multicols}
\z

We propose that all three tonal processes can be captured by 
a set of transduction relations mapping inputs to outputs. 
These input–output transductions differ only in the 
language-specific \textbf{reassociation} operation.

\paragraph{Logical Transduction of Tonal Processes}

Using model theory \citep{strother2019diss,yolyan2025logical,jardine2017local} 
we define a phonological representation as relational structures of the 
form $\langle D, \mathcal{R} \rangle$, where $D$ is a finite domain of 
positions, and $\mathcal{R}$ is a set of relations (also known as the
model \textit{signature}). 
An autosegmental representation (AR) is defined over a signature 
consisting of unary predicates, which indicate tonal or segmental 
properties, such as $H, L, V,$ and $C$, and binary relations that 
encode structural connections. In particular, we assume two binary 
relations: $\lhd$ and $\alpha$. The relation $\lhd$ encodes the successor relation: 
$\lhd(x,y)$ indicates that $x$ immediately follows $y$. 
For example, vowel hiatus can be defined as 
$\textsf{hiatus}(x,y) := V(x) \land V(y) \land \lhd(x,y)$. The relation $\alpha(x,y)$ encodes association, indicating 
that tone $x$ is linked to segment $y$.
These predicates, defined
in model-theoretic terms, express exactly the
same core ideas as autosegmental phonology \citep{goldsmith1976}, and reveal
greater simplicity as we delve deeper into tonal processes.

\input{ARmodels.tex}
Building on this view, we define a phonological transformation 
from an underlying representation (UR) to a surface 
representation (SR) is modeled as a relation between input
and output structures (see more in \citet{oakden2020notation}). 
The signature of the output can be obtained by referring to
the input signature. Formally, for any predicate $R(x)$ in the input signature,
a faithful output predicate $R'$ is defined as $R'(x) := R(x)$,
meaning that an element $x$ in the output domain has property $R$
iff it has property $R$ in the input.

\textbf{Our proposal} is that vowel-deletion–induced tonal 
processes preserve all structural relations in the AR except 
for $\alpha$, which is subject to language-specific 
\textit{faithfulness}, \textit{deassociation}, and 
\textit{reassociation}:
\(
\alpha'(x,y) := \bigl(\alpha(x,y)\land \neg \textsf{deassoc}(x,y)\bigr)
\lor \textsf{reassoc}(x,y)
\). The deleted units remains in the domain 
but are not realized at the surface, since a licensing function 
$\phi_{\textsf{license}}$  restricts realization to associated units.

Table~\ref{tab.transduction} lists all transductions from input 
to output. The licensing function ensures that only associated 
(i.e., fully specified) units surface in the SR. All unary 
relations are preserved from UR to SR, as is the successor 
relation $\lhd$. 

The output association relation $\alpha'$ is defined as follows: 
an association line holds in the output if (i) it is present 
in the input and not removed by \textsf{deassoc}, or (ii) it is 
introduced by \textsf{reassoc}. Concretely, \textsf{deassoc} 
removes the association between the deleted vowel and its tone. 
The language-specific \textsf{reassoc} operation then determines 
the outcome: it may be inactive (Engenni), reattach the tone to 
the surviving vowel (Estako), or rearrange association depends
on the tone quality (Ogoja Yala).

\textbf{(a) Tone deletion in Engenni} is derived by a single operation, 
\textsf{deassoc}, which deassociates the deleted 
vowel and its tone (indicated by the crossed-out line). This leaves 
both $T_1$ and $V_1$ unassociated. Since \textsf{reassoc} is inactive, 
no new association is formed. Consequently, $T_1$ and $V_1$ remain 
unassociated, therefore are not licensed by $\Phi_{license}$ 
(indicated in gray). As a result, only the second tone and vowel will surface.

\textbf{(b) Contour formation in Estako} is derived by the same 
\textsf{deassoc} operation. The \textsf{reassoc} operation then 
links the tone of the deleted vowel ($T_1$) to the surviving 
vowel ($V_2$). As a result, $V_2$ becomes associated with both 
$T_1$ and $T_2$, which forms a contour tone. At this stage, the 
only unassociated unit is $V_1$, which is therefore not licensed.
The surface form thus consists of the surviving vowel bearing 
a contour tone.

\textbf{(c) Mixed pattern in Ogoja Yala} provides strong evidence 
for the autosegmental nature of tone. From a logical perspective, 
the process closely parallels those in Engenni and Estako, with 
one key difference: \textsf{reassoc} applies only to H tones. 

If the deleted vowel $V_1$ is associated with a L tone, 
then it patterns with Engenni: only \textsf{deassoc} applies and 
\textsf{reassoc} remains inactive, leaving both elements 
unassociated and therefore not licensed. If the deleted vowel has 
an H tone, the process resembles Estako: the H tone reassociates 
to the surviving vowel ($V_2$), forming a contour tone. 

\textbf{Conclusion} This paper has argued that vowel-deletion–induced 
tonal processes can be uniformly captured as logical transductions 
over autosegmental representations (ARs). The observed typological 
variation follows from modifying a single relation, $\alpha$, through 
the interaction of \textsf{deassoc} and \textsf{reassoc}. 
A limitation of the current analysis is that the transduction relies 
on quantification to capture across-tier dependencies. An important 
direction for future work is to determine whether these processes can 
be simplified in a quantifier-free (QF) process.
This work also extends naturally to learning over ARs. Recent work 
by \citet{yolyan2025logical} demonstrates that logical transductions 
are learnable by using a bottom-up factorial inference algorithms 
\citep{chandleeBufia2019}.
